Music is often described as the most direct of the arts, but it may also be the most mediated by convention. A cadence is never only a cadence; it is a memory of other cadences. A melody is never entirely alone; it arrives with the ghosts of phrase, genre, taste, expectation, and social use.

The question, then, is not whether one can escape cliché. The question is whether one can make cliché truthful again. A musical language without inheritance is barely a language at all. But inheritance without pressure becomes upholstery: tasteful, comfortable, dead.

The artist’s task is perhaps not to be original in the vulgar sense, but to become answerable to the materials. To ask what they still know. To discover what they can be made to confess.
