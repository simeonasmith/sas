The thesis is a historical ethnography of music and colonialism that theorizes and documents a recursive

relationship of structuration between musical systems of composition and social systems of

power. Focusing on works by Samuel Coleridge-Taylor and Gustav Holst, the thesis explores how music-

theoretical concepts appropriated from African and Asian musical sources have been represented and

utilized within early-twentieth century modernist epistemologies. The main thrust of the thesis is to

understand how the social practice of composing in the early-twentieth century replicated, reflected,

renewed, and resisted the racialized and encultured structures of modernity/coloniality through the focal

lens of imperial London. The method pursued balances history with anthropology and uses techniques of

music analysis to draw specific conclusions about how social relations and cultural processes are embodied

and inscribed in musical systems and sonic phenomena.

The fundamental contention of the thesis is that subjectivity and agency within social structures

of modernity/coloniality were articulated in musical composition through two competing repertoires of

modernist practice: “modernism/expansionism” and “modernism/créolité.” Both modernisms emerge in

response to a critical awareness of Otherness (across colonial metropoles and peripheries alike) that

rendered the hierarchical orthodoxies of nineteenth-century Romanticism/imperialism unsustainable.

Modernism/expansionism, characterized as a metropolitan European response to the colonized Other, is

explored through the analysis of several works by Gustav Holst. By laying foundations for the

commoditization of musical subjectivity, I argue that Holst’s conceptualization of Otherness through

music-theoretical relativism and sonic fetishism had consequences on the expansion of coloniality under

globalized capitalism. On the other hand, modernism/créolité, as exemplified through close analysis of

the compositional practices of Samuel Coleridge-Taylor, is characterized as a resistive response to the

colonizer-Other from marginalized voices in the diaspora (and on the periphery), and reveals ‘musical

working’

as a practice for navigating the double consciousness of life under White and/or European

hegemony.

The thesis attempts to make a twofold scholarly contribution. (1) In the domain of social and

critical theory it complicates the macronarrative of modernity/coloniality by proposing it in terms of a

dialectic between modernism/expansionism and modernism/créolité. The dialectic imagines the radical

possibility of posing a threat to the structure from within the logic of the structure itself. (2) Its

contribution to music studies lies in a reimagining of the ‘musical work’ as a process of ‘musical working’

and advocating for musical composition as a deep inscription of structure and agency made accessible

through music analysis. I hope the legacy of the research is a renewed entwinement of embodied and

aesthetic approaches to music study.
